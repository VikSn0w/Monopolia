Monopolia: Training a Curriculum Reinforcement Learning Agent
on a Complete Monopoly Game Engine

Training dynamics, failure modes, and architectural fixes in a long-horizon stochastic board game

Independent Research — Monopolia Project

v1 Training Run · 18,000,000 total steps · MaskablePPO + Curriculum Learning

Abstract

I present Monopolia, a complete Monopoly game engine paired with a reinforcement learning agent trained via MaskablePPO under a four-stage difficulty curriculum. The agent operates on a 95-dimensional observation space and a 46-action discrete space, with invalid actions masked at every step. My first training run (v1, 18M total steps) revealed three critical failure modes: a degenerate policy in Stage 1 (100% win rate, ~85-step episodes) caused by trivially weak opponents; near-total truncation rates in Stages 2–4 (56–60%) caused by discount invisibility at high episode lengths; and insufficient step budgets for Stages 3–4, which had not yet converged. I quantitatively diagnose these failures and propose six architectural and training fixes constituting the v2 pipeline.

01Introduction

Monopoly is a canonical example of a long-horizon, high-variance, multi-agent board game. It combines stochastic elements (dice rolls, card draws) with strategic decisions (property acquisition, development, trading, debt management) spread across hundreds of turns. These properties make it a challenging but meaningful benchmark for reinforcement learning: the win signal is sparse, the game length is unbounded, and a naive agent must learn to convert early resource accumulation into a late-game victory condition.

Monopolia implements the complete official ruleset — including full trading mechanics (properties, cash, and Get Out of Jail Free cards), the even-build rule, mortgaging with interest, jail logic, and auction resolution — and wraps it in a Gymnasium-compatible environment for use with MaskablePPO from the sb3-contrib library. Action masking is critical here: at any given state the majority of the 46-action space is illegal, and allowing the policy to sample invalid actions would corrupt learning entirely.

This paper documents the v1 training run in full, presents the observed training dynamics and evaluation results, diagnoses the failure modes quantitatively, and describes the v2 fixes that will be applied in the next training cycle.

03Training Methodology

Training uses a four-stage curriculum implemented with MaskablePPO (sb3-contrib) and 8 parallel environments via make_vec_env. Each stage warms-starts from the previous model's weights with a reduced learning rate (1e-4) and entropy coefficient (0.005) to prevent catastrophic forgetting while still adapting to the harder opponent pool.

Core PPO hyperparameters: n_steps=4096, batch_size=512, n_epochs=10, ent_coef=0.01, clip_range=0.2, max_grad_norm=0.5, gae_lambda=0.95. The rollout buffer accumulates 4096 × 8 = 32,768 environment steps per gradient update, providing stable gradient estimates for this long-horizon game.

05Failure Mode Analysis

5.1 Stage 1 — Degenerate Policy

The flat reward plateau at ~+1,080 from 400k steps onward and the collapse of mean episode length to approximately 85 steps are unambiguous signs of a degenerate policy. A competitive Monopoly game against three opponents requires several hundred turns at minimum; 85 steps is consistent with the agent exploiting the trivially weak Difficulty 1 field by purchasing every reachable property and rolling without strategic consideration. This policy achieves 100% evaluation win rate against the same weak opponents but transfers almost no useful strategy to Stage 2.

💡

The warm-start from Stage 1 to Stage 2 transferred a shallow "buy everything, roll immediately" policy. The immediate reward cliff from ~+1,080 to ~+435 at Stage 2 onset is a direct consequence — the agent's learned behaviour does not generalise.

5.2 Stages 2–4 — Discount Invisibility

At γ = 0.99 and a mean episode length of 4,000 steps, the discount applied to the terminal win/loss reward is 0.994000 ≈ 1.6 × 10−18. This is below the numerical precision of 32-bit floats in practical terms — the terminal reward contributes effectively nothing to the gradient estimates for decisions made early in the game. The agent is learning exclusively from intermediate rewards (rent differentials, property acquisition), which are necessary but not sufficient conditions for winning.

This explains the dominant truncation pattern (56–60% of episodes in Stages 2–4 never terminate), the high reward variance (no stable win-signal to anchor the policy), and the lack of convergence visible in Stages 3 and 4's reward curves.

5.3 Stage 3–4 — Insufficient Step Budgets

Stage 3's reward curve at termination (5M steps, mean reward ~+131) shows an upward trend in the final 1M steps — the policy was still improving. Stage 4's peak reward of +162 at 5.2M steps followed by a decline to +49 at 6M steps suggests the policy found and then lost a local optimum, a common symptom of an under-trained warm-start combined with a too-aggressive learning rate during fine-tuning.

07Conclusion

The v1 training run produced a model that wins trivially against weak opponents (Stage 1: 100%) but fails to generalise to competitive play (Stages 2–4: 30–42% combined win rate, dominated by truncated episodes). The failure modes are well-understood and addressable: opponent diversity prevents degenerate policies, forced endgame converts long games into useful terminal signal, staged discounting matches γ to actual episode length, and extended budgets allow convergence for harder stages.

The v2 pipeline also introduces a full trading system — the final major missing mechanic — allowing the agent to negotiate directly for monopoly-completing properties via Action 45. This new degree of freedom, combined with the training fixes above, is expected to substantially improve the agent's strategic depth and win rates in the next training run. The target for public release of the RL codebase and trained models remains a Stage 2 combined win rate of 100%.
